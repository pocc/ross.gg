While writing a [travel blog](/posts/visiting-bermuda) about Bermuda, I became fascinated with why TLDs exist for parts of countries. I've asked about [TLDs vs effective TLDs](https://stackoverflow.com/questions/69418561/is-gov-uk-a-tld-or-a-domain) because DNS and TLD delegations are fascinating. We have TLDs like `.pr` for Puerto Rico and `.bm` for Bermuda even though they are a part of the US and UK respectively. The TLDs `.us` and `.uk` would make more sense. Most of the TLDs of the AI era, .ai, .sh, .io, and .ms, are actually TLDs for British subnational territories. Why is there a TLD for every dependent territory?

My hotel, Inverurie, like many businesses in Bermuda, proudly advertises its `.bm` domain. Despite being an island of 65,000 people, it gets its own TLD, just like other [British Overseas Territories](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/British_Overseas_Territories). 

I also venture into how these various British dependent territories have historically been tax havens as a tangent. **Independent, geographically distinct jurisdictions based on British Common Law are great tax havens prior to OECD pillars AND meet ISO 3166 standards for two-letter country codes.** Regardless, below, I take a look into the tax regimes and TLDs of various British Overseas Territories and Crown Dependencies.

## Why Subnational Territories Get Two-Letter TLDs

The reason two-letter TLDs are limited is due to country codes in [ISO 3166](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_ISO_3166_country_codes), which is just a mapping of countries to two and three-letter codes. Any subnational territory that has a separate administrative/legal system will be listed in this country code database, and thus be eligible for a two-letter TLD. 

Since 2012, brand TLDs or gTLD "Generic TLDs" not tied to a country have been [available for sale by ICANN](https://newgtlds.icann.org/en) to companies or governments with enough money. This still precludes two-letter TLDs though. 

## Dependent Territory TLDs and Tax Authorities

British dependent territories consist of British Overseas Territories (BOT) and Crown Dependencies (CD), but they are a part of the UK (UK international representation, but local autonomy). Their legal structure seems to be a vehicle for tax avoidance. Compare the US territory of Puerto Rico, which has a [corporate tax rate of 18.5%](https://taxsummaries.pwc.com/puerto-rico/corporate/taxes-on-corporate-income).

These subnational entities constitute 6 of the top 20 biggest enablers of tax abuse as of 2024, see https://cthi.taxjustice.net/ . If you can legally reside in these subnational jurisdictions, you could have a 0% personal income tax, but this is limited by the US and UK. 

An International Business Company is a tax vehicle for non-residents to do business outside of one of these subnational jurisdictions. The British Virgin Islands pioneered the idea of a "International Business company" via the [BVI IBC Act 1984](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_Business_Companies_Act), and many other former/current British colonies have copied this (including non-British colonies like the Seychelles and the Marshall Islands).

...So a two-letter TLD is a marker of being in ISO 3166, and ISO 3166 includes geographically distinct territories, dependencies, and special administrative regions. Bermuda fits this definition, like other British dependent territories.
